Responsive Web Design
Today's Internet users hear the acronym HTML and (maybe) understand it plays some role in the web pages they view and use. Though they understand HTML is a piece of their web experience, they are unfamiliar with how its fundamental role in modern web design is outdated and even somewhat unrelated to their experience today. Originally, HTML was designed for document exchange, management, and publishing. As the Internet has expanded, it's role in users' lives, culture, and society at large has evolved past document-sharing – though we do use the Internet for a significant amount of written content. In response to early changes, we developed Cascading Style Sheets – CSS – to alter the way pages look. Web Design is a specialization and direct result of this change in technology and adoption. Along with it, we developed language and ideas around best practices for users, developers, and designers. Following this major shift, the devices and software we use to discover content, interact with resources, and experience web pages have also dramatically evolved. As the ways we access and experience content have changed, so have the technical needs for and conceptual understanding of functional web design. Websites today exist and are accessed on different types of browsers, devices, and screen sizes. The field of Responsive Web Design has developed in response to the changing needs of both technology and users. Its premise is to design all web content around accessibility for a diverse range of devices, browsers, and users. It aims to overcome the constraints of designing for just one setting by creating templates that are flexible. By designing for different types of content – videos, images, and text – as well as different technology, web content can be richly experienced no matter the environment.
